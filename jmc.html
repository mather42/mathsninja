Q21 — Two nested squares PQRS and JKLM

21.1 (a) Prove PJ∶JQ = QK∶KR = RL∶LS = SM∶MP

Understand. JKLM is a smaller square drawn inside PQRS with each of its corners lying on one side of the big square.

Plan. Show that each corner of JKLM sits the same fraction λ of the way along its side of PQRS.

Do it.

  1. Let the length of PQ be 1 unit. Suppose point J divides PQ so that PJ = λ and JQ = 1 − λ.
  2. Because JKLM is a square, side JK is parallel to side PQ and every vertex of JKLM is reached by sliding the same fraction λ along a side of PQRS.
        Therefore on side QR we have QK = λ and KR = 1 − λ,
    and the same happens on RS and SP: RL = λ, LS = 1 − λ, SM = λ, MP = 1 − λ.
  3. Hence
    PJ∶JQ = λ∶(1−λ),
    QK∶KR = λ∶(1−λ),
    RL∶LS = λ∶(1−λ),
    SM∶MP = λ∶(1−λ).
    All four ratios are equal.

Look back. Sliding every corner the same fraction of its edge keeps the small square “in step” all the way round.

21.1 (b) Find λ if area JKLM : area PQRS = 25 : 49

Understand. Area compares as (side length)².

Plan. Take the square root of the area ratio to get the side-length ratio.

Do it.

  1. Small : big area = 25 : 49, so small side : big side = √25 : √49 = 5 : 7.
  2. Therefore the fraction we called λ is 5⁄7.

Look back. Whenever two similar shapes have an area ratio a : b, the matching length ratio is √a : √b.

Q23 — Three-town tour (roads in a fixed pattern)

23.1  The tour P → Q → R → P is 108 km – find each road length

Understand. The three direct roads are always in the ratio 5 : 4 : 3.

Plan. Add the ratio numbers (5 + 4 + 3 = 12). One “ratio part” times 12 gives the full tour length.

Do it.

  • 1 part = 108 km ÷ 12 = 9 km.
  • PQ = 5 parts = 5 × 9 = 45 km.
  • QR = 4 parts = 4 × 9 = 36 km.
  • RP = 3 parts = 3 × 9 = 27 km.

Look back. Dividing first, then multiplying, keeps the arithmetic simple and exact.

23.2  Extra rules
• Road lengths are whole numbers.
• P → R via Q is twice direct P → R.
• P → Q via R is three times direct P → Q.
Find the shortest possible tour.

Set up the equations.

  • Let direct PQ = x km, QR = y, RP = z.
  • P → R via Q is x + y; we’re told x + y = 2z.  ……(1)
  • P → Q via R is y + z; we’re told y + z = 3x.  ……(2)

Eliminate one letter.

  • From (1): y = 2z − x.
  • Put that into (2): (2z − x) + z = 3x ⇒ 3z − x = 3x ⇒ 3z = 4x ⇒ z = 4x⁄3.

Choose whole numbers. For z to be a whole number, x must be a multiple of 3. Write x = 3k (k is a positive integer).

  • Then z = 4k.
  • And y = 2z − x = 2·4k − 3k = 5k.

Tour length. x + y + z = 3k + 5k + 4k = 12k.

The smallest k = 1 gives 12 km. (x, y, z) = (3, 5, 4) works and no shorter whole-number choice exists.

23.3  New rules
• P → R via Q is three times direct P → R.
• P → Q via R is four times direct P → Q.
Again find the shortest tour.

New equations.

  • x + y = 3z.  ……(1′)
  • y + z = 4x.  ……(2′)

Solve as before.

  • From (1′): y = 3z − x.
  • Put into (2′): (3z − x) + z = 4x ⇒ 4z − x = 4x ⇒ 4z = 5x ⇒ z = 5x⁄4.
  • So x must be a multiple of 4. Let x = 4k.
  • Then z = 5k and y = 3z − x = 15k − 4k = 11k.

Tour length. x + y + z = 4k + 11k + 5k = 20k.
Smallest k = 1 gives 20 km.

Why this method works. Two tidy equations are enough; choosing the smallest “k” that keeps all three numbers whole gives the shortest tour.

Q24 — Numbers that leave remainder 5

24.1  List the positive integers that leave remainder 5 when dividing 180

Key idea (one line). If 180 ÷ n leaves 5, then 180 − 5 = 175 is exactly divisible by n.

Why subtract first?
It turns a “remainder” puzzle into a straight factor puzzle with one smaller number.

Step 1 – factor 175.

175 = 5 × 5 × 7

Step 2 – list all factors.

  • 1, 5, 7, 25, 35, 175.

Step 3 – remove any factor ≤ 5 (because n must be larger than the remainder 5 itself, or the remainder would be impossible).

The required numbers are 7, 25, 35, 175.

24.2  How many positive integers leave remainder 5 when dividing 365?

Step 1 – subtract the remainder.
365 − 5 = 360.

Step 2 – find every factor of 360. One tidy way is to list factor pairs:

  1 × 360   2 × 180   3 × 120   4 ×  90   5 ×  72
  6 ×  60   8 ×  45   9 ×  40  10 ×  36  12 ×  30
 15 ×  24  18 ×  20

There are 12 pairs, which makes 24 factors altogether.

Step 3 – throw away any factor ≤ 5. Those are 1, 2, 3, 4, 5 (five numbers).

Step 4 – final count. 24 factors minus the 5 too-small ones → 19 numbers.

Why list pairs?
Writing factors in pairs guarantees you don’t miss any and stops as soon as the first number in a pair passes the square root (here √360 ≈ 19).

Q25 — Triangles whose corners are vertices of a cube

25.1  How many right-angled triangles?

Understand. A cube has 8 vertices. We must choose any 3 and see whether the triangle they form is right-angled.

Plan.

  1. Write down the three different edge-lengths that can occur between two cube vertices.
  2. Use Pythagoras (a² + b² = c²) to spot right triangles.
  3. Count how many of each type there are.

Step 1 – distance “catalogue”.

SegmentReal lengthSquare of length
Edge (two corners joined by one cube edge)11
Face diagonal (across one square face)√22
Space diagonal (corner to opposite corner of cube)√33

Step 2 – possible right-triangle shapes.

  • Type A: edge, edge, face-diagonal (squared: 1 + 1 = 2). Right angle sits at a cube corner.
  • Type B: edge, face-diagonal, space-diagonal (1 + 2 = 3). Right angle also at a cube corner.

Step 3 – counting each type.

  • Type A (edge–edge). Pick any of the 8 corners; three edges meet there, and you can choose any two of them (3 ways). 8 corners × 3 choices = 24.
  • Type B (edge + face diagonal). At a corner there are again 3 edges. For every edge there is exactly one face-diagonal through the same corner that is at right angles to that edge. So 3 right-angle choices per corner, just like before. 8 × 3 = 24.

Total right-angled triangles = 24 + 24 = 48.

Look back. Pythagoras becomes quick once you list the three possible squared lengths (1, 2, 3) only once.

25.2  How many isosceles triangles?

Understand. An isosceles triangle has two equal sides (they can be any of the three lengths in the table).

Plan.

  1. All 24 of the Type A right-angled triangles already have two equal sides (edge, edge, face-diagonal).
  2. Look for any other triangles with two equal edges among the vertex set.

Extra isosceles set – the “diamond” on each face.

Pick one square face of the cube. Its four corners form two face diagonals. Choose one diagonal and one of the two remaining adjacent vertices: that triangle has sides √2, √2, √2 (an equilateral triangle lying flat on the face). There are 2 such triangles on each of the 6 faces → 12. But each of those equilateral triangles belongs to two faces, so we would double-count if we multiplied by 6. The correct count is therefore 8 equilateral triangles in total.

Add everything.

  • Right-angled isosceles (Type A) = 24
  • Equilateral √2 triangles = 8

Total isosceles triangles = 24 + 8 = 32.

Look back. The “distance catalogue then filter” trick avoids messy 3-D drawings—once all lengths are known, the classification is mechanical.

Subtract the remainder first, then find factors or make a short table Q22 & Q24 Turns “remainder” puzzles into ordinary factor checks with smaller numbers.

General remarks for 2025.
  • Pólya in action: You can spot the “Plan” phase quickly—subtract, divide, or catalogue distances before any heavy calculation.
  • When only a few integer candidates exist (Q22), a small test table is faster than inventing new symbols.
  • A single clever sketch (big vs. small square, cube with labelled edges) often replaces pages of algebra.
  • Always finish with a short “look-back”: try an extreme value, count all cases, or check unit consistency. Mistakes show up here with very little extra work.

Q21 — The patchwork rectangle

21.1  Fraction of PQRS that is the large unshaded square

The picture: the big rectangle PQRS is tiled by 8 small squares, 5 shaded (side y) and 3 unshaded (side x). We are told 5x = 3y.

Step 1 – express every side with one letter.

  • x = 3y⁄5 (from 5x = 3y).
  • Width of PQRS = 4x + y = 4·(3y⁄5) + y = 12y⁄5 + 5y⁄5 = 17y⁄5.
  • Height of PQRS = 3y.

Step 2 – area of PQRS. (17y⁄5) × (3y) = 51y²⁄5.

Step 3 – area of the large unshaded square. Its side is 4x = 4·(3y⁄5) = 12y⁄5, so area = (12y⁄5)² = 144y²⁄25.

Step 4 – form the fraction.

(area big unshaded) ÷ (area PQRS)
= (144y²⁄25) ÷ (51y²⁄5)
= (144/25) × (5/51) = 720/1275 = 48⁄85.

Answer: 48⁄85.

21.2  Ratio of a small unshaded square to a shaded square

Step 1 – write both areas.

  • Small unshaded square = x².
  • Shaded square = y².

Step 2 – link x and y. From 5x = 3y ⇒ x = 3y⁄5.

Step 3 – form the ratio.

x² : y² = (3y⁄5)² : y² = 9 : 25.

Answer: 9 : 25.

Why this method?
• Turn every unknown side into one letter (here y).
• Work with areas last; most fractions then collapse cleanly.
• Remember: area-ratio = (side-ratio)² for similar squares.

Q23 — A square and a rectangle share a clever area link

23.1  The square’s perimeter when its area is seven times that of the rectangle

Problem restated in plain words.
• Let the square’s side be s cm.
• The rectangle is one third as long as the square and
   its width is 7 cm less than the square’s side.

1 Understand.

  • Square area = s².
  • Rectangle length = s⁄3.
  • Rectangle width = s − 7.
  • The square’s area is 7 × rectangle’s area.

2 Plan (one clean equation).
Write square area = 7 × rectangle area, then solve for s. Finally multiply by 4 for the perimeter.

3 Do it step-by-step.

Area equation

s² = 7 × (s⁄3)(s − 7)

Clear the fraction (multiply both sides by 3)

3s² = 7s(s − 7)

Expand and tidy

3s² = 7s² − 49s  ⇒  4s² − 49s = 0

Factor out s

s(4s − 49) = 0

Ignore s = 0 (side length can’t be 0)

4s − 49 = 0  ⇒  s = 49⁄4 = 12.25 cm

Perimeter of the square

4 × 12.25 cm = 49 cm

4 Look back.

  • We solved with one tidy equation—no need for separate variables for the rectangle.
  • Divide by s early (after checking s ≠ 0) to keep numbers small.
  • Extreme test: if the “7 cm less” were “0 cm less,” the rectangle would equal the square and the factor 7 would fail—good reason to trust our algebra.
Why write just one equation?
Introducing extra letters (for rectangle area or perimeter) only creates more work. A single, carefully built area equation is quicker and less error-prone.

Q25 — Right-handed, left-handed and blue eyes

25.1  Fraction of right-handers who have blue eyes

Problem facts.

  • Eight-ninths of the pupils are right-handed.
  • One-ninth are left-handed (because 1 − 8⁄9 = 1⁄9).
  • One-sixth of the left-handers have blue eyes.
  • Altogether, one-quarter of all pupils have blue eyes.
  • Question: what fraction of the right-handers have blue eyes?

1 Understand. Working with one big number can be messy, so treat the whole school as “1 unit”. Then every statement above becomes a simple fraction.

2 Plan.

  1. Write an equation: (blue-eyed left-handers) + (blue-eyed right-handers) = 1⁄4.
  2. We already know the first bracket; call the second bracket x (the answer we need).

3 Do it step-by-step.

  • Left-handed fraction = 1⁄9.
  • Blue-eyed left-handers = (1⁄6 of 1⁄9) = 1⁄54.
  • Right-handed fraction = 8⁄9.
  • Let x = fraction of right-handers with blue eyes.
    Then blue-eyed right-handers = (8⁄9) × x.

Balance of blue eyes for the whole school

1⁄54  +  (8⁄9)·x = 1⁄4

Work it out.

(8⁄9)·x = 1⁄4 − 1⁄54
    = (27⁄108) − (2⁄108) = 25⁄108

x = (25⁄108) × (9⁄8)  =  225⁄864  =  25⁄96.

Answer: 25⁄96 of the right-handed pupils have blue eyes.

4 Look back.

  • Working with “1 unit” avoided hunting for a common total.
  • If only 1⁄5 of all pupils had blue eyes the same method would still work—just change the final 1⁄4.
Why a single equation?
Writing “fraction of the whole = part + part” is quicker (and safer) than separate percentage trees.

Q21 — Sheila’s Sheep Shop

21.1  How many sheep does Sheila have in total?

Information given.

  • Cheviots → Suffolks → Dorsets → Herdwicks form a ratio chain.
  • Six times as many Suffolks as Cheviots.
  • Dorsets are twice the number of Suffolks.
  • Herdwicks are half the number of Dorsets.
  • There are 20 more Herdwicks than Cheviots.

1 Understand. Work up the chain starting from the smallest group (Cheviots).

2 Plan. Call the number of Cheviots C. Express every other breed in terms of C.

3 Do it step-by-step.

  • Cheviots = C
  • Suffolks = 6C  (“six times”)
  • Dorsets = 12C  (double the Suffolks)
  • Herdwicks = 6C  (half the Dorsets)

Use the final clue.

Herdwicks = Cheviots + 20
6C = C + 20  ⇒  5C = 20  ⇒  C = 4

Find every breed.

  • Cheviots = 4
  • Suffolks = 6 × 4 = 24
  • Dorsets = 12 × 4 = 48
  • Herdwicks = 6 × 4 = 24

Total sheep.

4 + 24 + 48 + 24 = 100

4 Look back.

  • The ratio ladder (multiply then divide) avoided simultaneous equations.
  • Always start the ladder with the breed that has a direct “+ 20” or “× 6” clue.
  • Quick check: Herdwicks – Cheviots = 24 − 4 = 20 ✔︎
Habit highlighted.
Ratio ladder. Start at the smallest unit, climb up one rung at a time using the given multipliers or dividers, then plug in the final “add 20” type clue.

Q22 — 3 × 3 product grid with the numbers 1 to 9

22.1  Show that one filling is possible

Understand. We must place the digits 1-9 so the row-products are 18, 105, 192 and the column-products are 56, 180, 36.

Plan. Use the anchor-prime idea: if a prime shows up in exactly one row-product and one column-product, its cell is fixed.

Do.

  • Prime 7 appears only in 56 (column 1) and 105 (row 2) → put 7 at (row 2, col 1).
  • Prime 5 appears only in 105 (row 2) and 180 (column 2) → put 5 at (row 2, col 2).
  • Divide the affected products:
    • C1 becomes 56 ÷ 7 = 8; R2 becomes 105 ÷ (7·5)=3; C2 becomes 180 ÷ 5=36.
  • Now 8 (C1) appears only in R3’s 192 → place 8 at (R3,C1). Continue dividing; each blank becomes forced.

Resulting grid:

192
753
846

Look back. Row check → 18, 105, 192; column check → 56, 180, 36. All digits 1-9 used once, so the placement works.

22.2  Two new grids – decide if they are possible

(a)  Row 80 / 189 / 24  •  Column 56 / 45 / 144

Understand. Same task with a new set of six products.

Plan. Apply the anchor-prime test again.

Do.

  • Prime 7 only in 56 (C1) & 189 (R2) → 7 at (R2,C1).
  • Prime 5 only in 45 (C2) & 80 (R1) → 5 at (R1,C2).
  • Divide and finish — every remaining blank is forced.

One valid grid:

258
793
416

Look back. Row check → 80, 189, 24; column check → 56, 45, 144 – so this grid is possible.

(b)  Row 72 / 140 / 36  •  Column 48 / 168 / 90

Understand. Same rules, different numbers.

Plan. Try the anchor-prime test first; if it fails, look for a contradiction.

Do.

  • Prime 7 appears in every row-product and in two column-products (168, 90). That would force the digit 7 to occupy at least three different cells — impossible with only one 7.

Look back. No arrangement can work; the grid is impossible.

Anchor-prime habit.
• Prime shows up once in a row-product & once in a column-product → cell fixed.
• Prime shows up many times → likely clash → quick impossibility proof.

Q23 — Regular-pentagon geometry

23.1  Making the “symmetry” precise

The picture shows a regular pentagon PQRST with centre O. Points H, I, J, … mark feet of perpendiculars from O to the sides, forming five equal 60° wedges at the centre.

(a)  Prove triangles QOP and TOP are congruent

1 Understand.

  • Both triangles share side OP.
  • OQ = OT (radii of the same circle).
  • QP = PT (all edges of a regular pentagon are equal).

2 Plan. Use the SSS rule (three equal sides give congruent triangles).

3 Do it.

• Side 1: OQ = OT (radii)
• Side 2: QP = PT (equal pentagon edges)
• Side 3: OP common to both triangles

Therefore △QOP ≅ △TOP by SSS.

4 Look back. Congruent triangles give equal matching angles: ∠QPO = ∠TPO and ∠QOP = ∠TOP.

(b)  Deduce ∠SHO = ∠RHO = 90°

1 Key observation. Quadrilateral PTSH is a kite:

  • PS = PT (pentagon sides).
  • HS = HT (radii drop perpendicular to sides in a regular pentagon).

2 Kite fact. In a kite, the line through the equal sides (P–H here) is a line of symmetry; it meets the other diagonal at a right angle.

3 Apply the kite fact.

The diagonals PH and SO cross at H, so ∠SHO = 90°.

4 Parallel reasoning in quadrilateral PQRH.

  • PQ = PR and HQ = HR (again a kite).
  • The two diagonals meet at 90° ⇒ ∠RHO = 90°.

5 Conclusion. Both angles are right angles, making the earlier “by symmetry” statement precise.

Habits highlighted.
• Replace “looks symmetrical” with a congruence proof.
• Spot kites in a regular polygon; their diagonals meet at right angles, a fact that often turns hand-waving into a solid argument.

23.2  Interior-angle formula and quick checks

(a)  Derive a formula for an n-gon

Idea. From one vertex draw diagonals to every other non-adjacent vertex. That cuts the n-gon into n − 2 triangles.

Total angle sum

(n − 2) × 180°  =  (n − 2)·180°
(b)  Check that a quadrilateral’s angles sum to 360°

Put n = 4.

(4 − 2)·180° = 2·180° = 360°
(c)  Check a regular pentagon’s interior angle is 108°

Put n = 5.

(5 − 2)·180° = 3·180° = 540°.
Divide by 5 equal angles ⇒ 540° ÷ 5 = 108°
Habit highlighted.
Slicing any n-gon from one vertex into (n − 2) triangles is the quickest way to remember or re-derive angle sums.

Q25 — Granny’s gifts (spoons → cats → teapots)

25.1  Check the algebra line in Method 1

Understand. Method 1 repeatedly keeps ⅔ of what is left, then subtracts 8. After three rounds the booklet states

\[ \frac23\!\Bigl(\frac23\!\bigl(\tfrac23 x - 8 \bigr)\;-\;8 \Bigr)\;-\;8 \;=\; \frac{8}{27}\,x\;-\;\frac{152}{9}. \]

Plan. Expand step-by-step and simplify.

Do.

  1. First step \(\displaystyle \tfrac23 x - 8\).
  2. Second  \(\displaystyle \tfrac23\bigl(\tfrac23 x - 8\bigr) - 8 = \tfrac49 x - \tfrac{16}{3} - 8 = \tfrac49 x - \tfrac{40}{3}.\)

25.3  General teapot formula

(a)  Derive x in terms of k and p

Understand. Now she gives "k more than \(\dfrac1p\) of what remains" three times.

Plan. Forward step multiplies by \(a=\tfrac{p-1}{p}\) and subtracts k. Reverse step multiplies by \(\tfrac{p}{p-1}\) and adds k.

Do.

  • Let \(a=\tfrac{p-1}{p}\). Forward formula: new total = \(a\,x - k\).
  • Three forward steps give \(a^{3}x - k(a^{2}+a+1)=0\).
  • Therefore \[ \boxed{\,x = k\,\frac{a^{2}+a+1}{a^{3}} = k\,p\,\frac{3p^{2}-3p+1}{(p-1)^{3}}\,}. \]

Look back. Dimensions check: if k = 0 (she just gives perfect fractions) the formula reduces to \(x = p^{3}/(p-1)^{3}\), as expected from classic “p−1 over p” problems.

(b)  Substitute k = 8, p = 3 (spoons) → 57
\(a = \tfrac23;\; a^{2}+a+1 = \tfrac{13}{9};\; a^{3} = \tfrac{8}{27}\)
\(x = 8·3·\dfrac{13}{9} ÷ \dfrac{8}{27} = 57.\) ✔︎
(c)  Substitute k = 27, p = 4 (cats) → 148
\(a = \tfrac34;\; a^{2}+a+1 = \tfrac{37}{16};\; a^{3} = \tfrac{27}{64}\)
\(x = 27·4·\dfrac{37}{16} ÷ \dfrac{27}{64} = 148.\) ✔︎

Look back. Both substitutions agree with earlier answers, confirming the formula.

Habits from Q25.
• For multi-stage “give away” puzzles, work backwards with the inverse fraction.
• Write the forward step once, then cube (or square) it instead of rewriting three separate stages.
• Always verify a general formula by plugging in the booklet’s special cases.

Q23 — Train-ticket price changes

23.1  A 20 % rise followed by a discount leaves the ticket £2.38 cheaper than before

Understand.
• The original fare is £23.80.
• First it rises by 20 %.
• Then a special-offer discount pulls the price down so that the final cost is £2.38 below the original £23.80.
• We must find that discount as a percentage of the price after the rise.

Plan.
1  Compute the exact price after the 20 % rise.
2  Compute the exact final price (original – £2.38).
3  Turn “new ÷ old” into a multiplier and then into a percentage cut.

Do.

  1. After the 20 % rise. A 20 % increase means multiply by 1.20:
    £23.80 × 1.20 = £28.56.
  2. After the discount. The question says the final ticket is £2.38 cheaper than the original, so
    £23.80 – £2.38 = £21.42.
  3. Find the discount multiplier.
    multiplier = (final price) ÷ (price after rise)   = 21.42 ÷ 28.56 ≈ 0.75.
  4. Convert to a percentage cut.
    A multiplier of 0.75 means the fare is 75 % of what it was, so the cut is 25 %.

Look back.
• 25 % of £28.56 is exactly £7.14, and £28.56 – £7.14 = £21.42, so the arithmetic checks out.
• The cut is larger than the earlier rise (25 % vs 20 %), which makes sense because the final fare ends up below the starting £23.80.

23.2  After a 20 % rise, a protest brings the price down so it is only 5 % above the original

Understand.
• Let the original price be £p (we do not need its actual number).
• First rise: multiply by 1.20.
• Second change (the protest cut): ends at 5 % above £p, i.e. at 1.05 × p.
• We need the percentage reduction of that second change.

Plan.
Treat the unknown percentage reduction as a multiplier k. Build one chain equation and solve for k, then translate k into a percentage cut.

Do.

After rise: 1.20 p
After cut: 1.20 p × k = 1.05 p

Solve for k: k = 1.05⁄1.20 = 0.875.

Convert: 0.875 = 87.5 % of the pre-cut price → the cut is 12.5 %.

Look back.
• 12½ % of £28.56 is £3.57, and £28.56 – £3.57 ≈ £24.99, which is indeed 5 % above £23.80.
• Checking with the abstract symbols first kept the algebra tiny, then one numerical check confirmed the sanity.

Habit ⚔️ Chain-multipliers.
• Always line up each percentage change as a multiplier (1 + rise or 1 – cut).
• Unknown cuts become a single letter multiplier; solving one equation beats juggling two percentage phrases.

Q24 — Fleur’s Flower-shop ratios

24.1  How many pot plants are in the shop?

Understand.
Four plant types and three linked ratios:

  • Azaleas : Begonias = 1 : 3
  • Begonias : Cyclamen = 2 : 5
  • Cyclamen : Dracaenas = 4 : 7

We need the total number of plants, knowing it is **below 300**.

Plan.
Build a ratio ladder starting from Azaleas (A): climb up, clearing any fractions, until all four numbers are integers. Then scale that whole set by a positive integer k and pick the largest k with a total < 300.

Do.

  1. First rung. A : B = 1 : 3 ⇒ B = 3A.
  2. Second rung. B : C = 2 : 5 ⇒ C = (5/2) B = (5/2)·3A = 15/2 A. To keep C an integer, **A must be even**.
  3. Third rung. C : D = 4 : 7 ⇒ D = (7/4) C = (7/4)·15/2 A = 105/8 A. For D to be an integer, **A must be a multiple of 8**.
  4. Choose the smallest such A. Let A = 8.
    ⇒ B = 24, C = 60, D = 105.
  5. Total for one full ratio unit. 8 + 24 + 60 + 105 = **197**.
  6. Scale by k. 2 × 197 = 394 (> 300) so k = 1 is the only option.

Answer. Fleur has 197 pot plants.

Look back.
• 197 < 300 satisfies the “fewer than 300” condition.
• Any smaller k would break an earlier rung; any larger k overshoots 300.

Habit ⚔️ Ratio ladder. Start at the smallest unknown, multiply or divide one step at a time, and note when the next rung forces the first number to be a multiple of 2, 4, 8, … A single scale factor k then sweeps all larger totals at once.

Q25 — Five-row and six-row number pyramids

25.1  Five-row pyramid, bottom-row sum 17

Understand.
Bottom row is v w x y z, all different positive integers, total 17. The middle three equations given in the main solution are:

w + x = 6   x + y = 7   v + w + x + y + z = 17

Plan.
Use the three equations to pin down each letter, then count the possible orderings of the five numbers left – right.

Do.

  1. Subtract the first two equations → y − w = 1.
  2. Add the first two → 2x + w + y = 13.
    But y = w + 1, so 2x + 2w + 1 = 13 ⇒ 2x + 2w = 12 ⇒ x + w = 6 (already known).
  3. This forces x = 4, w = 2, y = 3.
  4. Sum so far 2 + 4 + 3 = 9; hence v + z = 17 − 9 = 8.
  5. v and z must be positive, distinct, and different from 2, 3, 4.
    The only unordered pair is {1, 7}. Two orderings give (v,z) = (1,7) or (7,1).
  6. The bottom row order v-w-x-y-z contains five slots. x, w, y are locked to the middle three, but v and z can go left or right → 6 distinct arrangements in total.

Look back. A forward rebuild of the pyramid from each of the six admissible rows gives positive integers throughout, confirming our count.

25.2  Six-row pyramid — expression for the top cell

Understand.
Each brick is the sum of the two bricks below. With six bottom numbers u v w x y z, the top coefficient pattern is the fifth row of Pascal’s triangle (because we add up five times).

Plan. List the binomial coefficients for row 5: 1 5 10 10 5 1.

Do.
\[ \text{Top} = u + 5v + 10w + 10x + 5y + z. \]

Numerical check. Put
u = 25, v = 40, w = 72, x = 84, y = 42, z = 27.

25 + 5·40 + 10·72 + 10·84 + 5·42 + 27 = 2022.

Look back. Coefficients sum to 32 (= 2⁵), matching the fact each original number is used in 32 different paths to the top brick.

25.3  What do you notice about the coefficients?

Understand. The coefficients in the top four rows of any “add-the-two-below” pyramid are:

Row from bottomCoefficients

Plan & Do. Recognise these rows as successive lines of Pascal’s triangle.

Look back. Every time we move up a layer, each new coefficient is the sum of the two just below it—exactly how Pascal’s triangle is built. That explains why the binomial pattern appears automatically in any addition pyramid.

Habit ⚔️  When a pyramid rule is “add the two below,” expect Pascal numbers; use them to write the top in closed form and to spot hidden symmetries.

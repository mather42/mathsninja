Q21 — Tilted square inside a square

1 Understand

You have a big square. On each side you mark the same fraction along  – call that fraction f. Join the marks; they make a smaller, tilted square. How big is that inner square?

2 Plan

Because all four corners look the same, the picture is very symmetrical. A neat way to find the inner area is:

Big square area – area of the four identical corner triangles.

3 Do it

  • Side of big square = 1 (we can choose 1 to make life easy).
  • One corner triangle has sides f and 1 – f and is right-angled.
  • Triangle area = ½ × f × (1 – f).
  • Four triangles together: 2 f (1 – f).
  • So inner square area = 1 – 2f (1 – f) = 1 – 2f + 2f².

Check the special case in the booklet: if f = ⅓, the answer is 1 – 2 × ⅓ × ⅔ = 5⁄9, exactly as printed.

4 Look back

  • Extreme test: f = 0 or 1 gives inner area 1 – the formula works.
  • The area is smallest when f = ½; only half the big square is left.
  • Big idea learned: when a picture is strongly symmetrical, it’s often easier to remove repeated bits than to tackle the awkward shape head-on.

Q22 — Sweets counted in 4s and 5s

1 Understand

Idil has 31 toffees.
• When she makes piles of 4, three are left over.
• When she makes piles of 5, one is left over.

How many fudges (the leftovers) does she have? Then, what if she had 32 toffees instead?

2 Plan

Handle leftovers by trial + tidy sums:

  1. Remove the leftovers from 31.
  2. The number that remains must fit exactly into both pile sizes.

3 Do it

Fudges (groups of 5)Toffees usedLeftMultiple of 4?
0031No (7 r 3)
1526No (6 r 2)
21021No (5 r 1)
31516Yes (4×4)

So Idil has 3 fudges and 16 mints.

For 32 toffees repeat quicker: 32 – 5y must be a multiple of 4. y = 4 works ⇒ 32 – 20 = 12 ⇒ 3 groups of 4. Idil would have 4 fudges.

  • Big idea: “Left over” → strip that bit first, then check what’s left.
Q23 — Three-leg walk (ratio 5 : 4 : 3)

1 Understand

Legs PQ : QR : RP = 5 : 4 : 3.
(a) Total distance = 108 km.
(b) Shorten RP by k %. What happens to the perimeter?

2 Plan

Use the one-part method: add ratio numbers, divide the total, then multiply.

3 Do it

(a) 5 + 4 + 3 = 12 parts.
108 ÷ 12 = 9 km per part.

  • PQ = 5 × 9 = 45 km
  • QR = 4 × 9 = 36 km
  • RP = 3 × 9 = 27 km

(b) New RP = 27 × (100 – k)/100.
New perimeter = 45 + 36 + that number. Give it any target total and solve for k with basic percent algebra.

4 Look back

  • Ratio habit: divide first, multiply later.
  • Any fixed-ratio family stretches or shrinks together.
Q24 — Divisors leaving remainder 5 when dividing 180

1 Understand

We need every integer n > 5 such that 180 ÷ n leaves remainder 5.

2 Plan

Subtract the remainder first: 180 – 5 = 175. List factors of 175 and keep those > 5.

3 Do it

175 = 5²×7 ⇒ factors 1, 5, 7, 25, 35, 175.
Discard 1 and 5 → 7, 25, 35, 175.

4 Look back

  • “Subtract the remainder” is the universal shortcut for these problems.
  • After that, it’s ordinary factor hunting.

Q25 — Counting right-angled & isosceles triangles in a cube

1 Understand

Select any 3 of the 8 cube vertices.

2 Plan

Catalogue squared edge lengths (1, 2, 3) and apply Pythagoras / equal-side tests.

3 Do it

Right-angled.

  • (1, 1, 2) type: 3 per corner × 8 = 24.
  • (1, 2, 3) type: 3 per corner × 8 = 24.
  • Total = 48.

Isosceles.

  • The 24 right-angled (1, 1, 2) are already isosceles.
  • Add 8 equilateral √2–√2–√2 on faces → 32.

4 Look back

  • 56 choose-3 triangles exist; 48 + 8 = 56 – sanity check passes.
  • Catalogue → filter is unbeatable for regular lattices.

Q21 — The patchwork rectangle (parts 21.1 & 21.2)

21.1 Fraction of the big rectangle that is the large unshaded square

  1. Understand. The big rectangle is split into 8 squares. We know 5x = 3y, where x = side of a small unshaded square, y = side of a shaded square.
  2. Plan. Express every area in terms of one letter (use y) and take a simple fraction.
  3. Do it.
    • Large unshaded square side = 4x = 4·(3y/5) = 12y/5 → area = (12y/5)² = 144y²/25.
    • Whole rectangle: width 4x+y = 17y/5, height 3y → area = 17·3 y²/5 = 51y²/5.
    $$\text{Fraction}=\frac{144/25}{51/5}=\frac{144}{25}\times\frac{5}{51}= \frac{720}{1275}=\boxed{\tfrac{48}{85}}.$$
  4. Look back. Cancelling by 15 is a good mental check. Habit learned: reduce everything to one “unit side” before comparing areas.

21.2 Ratio of a small unshaded square to a shaded square

  1. Understand. Compare areas x² (small unshaded) and y² (shaded).
  2. Plan. Use x=3y/5 again.
  3. Do it. $$x^{2}:y^{2}=\bigl(\tfrac35y\bigr)^{2}:y^{2}=9:25.$$
  4. Look back. An area-ratio is the square of a side-ratio. Keep that fact handy.

Q23 — Pentagon geometry

23.1 — Prove ∠SHO = ∠RHO = 90°

StepReasoning
UnderstandRegular pentagon ⇒ equal sides/angles, centre O gives five 72° sectors.
PlanShow two triangles are SSS congruent; use them inside kites whose diagonals are ⟂.
Do ΔQOP and ΔTOP: OQ = OT (radii), OP common, QP = PT (sides of pentagon) ⇒ congruent. Hence ∠QPO = ∠TPO. Quadrilateral PT SH is a kite (two equal pairs) ⇒ diagonals perpendicular ⇒ ∠SHO = 90°. Same for PQRH ⇒ ∠RHO = 90°.
Look backWhat seemed “magic symmetry” is just SSS + kite facts.

23.2 — Interior-angle-sum formula

  1. Slice any n-gon into (n−2) triangles ⇒ sum = (n−2)·180°.
  2. Quadrilateral (n = 4) ⇒ 360°.
  3. Pentagon (n = 5) ⇒ 540° total, 108° each.
